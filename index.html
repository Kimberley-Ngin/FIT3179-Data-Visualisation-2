The Black Summer: 2019-20 Australian Bushfires

Summer of 2019-20 marks one of the most catastrophic bushfire seasons recorded in Australia. This visualisation will walk you through the factors leading up to the event, as well as paint a picture of the aftermath, telling the story of how the 2019-20 bushfires devastated Australia.

1

Rising Temperatures

Temperature is a significant factor in fire susceptibility. With global surface temperatures on the rise, Australia is no exception. The country is continuing to experience intense heat waves and rising average annual temperatures, driving the worsening of bushfire activity.

Leading up to the 2019-20 bushfires, average annual temperatures reached an all-time high, placing 2019 as the hottest year recorded thus far.

Rainfall Deficiency

Risk of forest fire is further exacerbated by drier conditions and reduction in rainfall. Not only is Australia getting hotter, the country is also experiencing rainfall deficiencies in recent years. This drought is characterised by three consecutive dry winters in 2017, 2018, and 2019, particularly in the southeast regions of Australia.

This trend of declining annual rainfall over time further increases risk of severe fire weather.

3

The Worst Bushfire Season

Severe fire weather aggravated by climate change is causing more extreme fires and longer bushfire seasons. In the summer of 2019-20, bushfires were ablaze, igniting in the north of Australia, and migrating toward the southeast regions. Over 24 million hectares of land was burned, with New South Wales experiencing its worst bushfire season.

Impact

New South Wales was hit the hardest with 25% of forest area burned, and not far behind was Victoria with 18% of forest area burned. Native forest occupied a large majority of the affected forest.

Approximately 60% of Australian adults were exposed to bushfire smoke in the summer of 2019-20. Pollution from the bushfires resulted in an estimated 2,027 people being admitted to hospital with respiratory problems, and 1,305 people with asthma-like conditions admitted to the emergency department.
